Analytics
From Feb 10 to Feb 18, we used Google Analytics to track website performance and evaluate feature performance using A/B testing methodology.

Website Performance Overview

60.5% were customers from direct channel while 39.5% were referred customers.
Conversion rate of referred customers (0.54%) outperformed that of direct customers (0.29%) by 86.20%.

Source Break-Down

Referred customers were from Yale University, School of Management, Yale Law School, Medicine and Divinity School, etc.
Conversation rate of referred customers from Medicine, Law and Divinity and Scrum Master were significantly higher than other referral or direct sources.

A/B Test

RSVP Experiment We use click through rate of event details page (measured by conversion rate) to track feature's performance. From Feb 17 to Feb 18, the non-RSVP feature (2.56%) performed worse than RSVP feature (3.03%). However, if we track by 'Make a Donation Goal Conversion Rate', the non-RSVP actually performed better (4.27%) compared to RSVP (3.79%). This somehow means that there are more people who continue to click on event details when they see RSVP in the event link but these people do not necessarily donate more.
We thought this discrepancy between conversion rate and 'Make a Donation Goal Conversion Rate' might be caused by two reasons:
1. Uneven distribution of experiments
2. Customers who clicked through event detail pages were less likely to donate

Note: Original - with RSVP in event links. RSVP - without RSVP in event links. Our apologies for the confusion in naming our variations.


Donate Experiment In the same time frame, "DONATE" feature outperformed "SUPPORT" feature measured by 'Make a Donation Goal Conversation Rate' as well.
The conversion rate of Original (with donate feature) page was 8.96% compared to variation 1 (with support feature) which was only 5.26%.

Recommendations
We recommend adding RSVP in the event links to attract more traffic into event details page and asking people to "DONATE" in order to achieve a higher donation percentage from users.
Retrospective
We structured our Retrospective into identifying what we could Start, Stop, and Continue doing.
  • Start doing: making the scrum master a full-time role
    Currently, the team relies on one group member for technical support. While the team member is an outstanding asset to the team, she inevitably takes a heavier workload. Moving forward, we should set aside the position of a scrum master to avoid overburdening the technical person.

  • Stop doing: clearing all the user stories in the initial stage
    The team finished all the user stories and passed all the mocha tests at the end of sprint 2. While this allows sufficient time for the team to deal with the curveballs, we can perhaps go with a more distributed workload across time.

  • Continue doing: update each other on unexpected delays via email
    The team just evolved in a way that team members are very good with communicating each other about unexpected delays via emails. This way, the team can adjust and re-estimate work progress before the sprint meeting and take corresponding actions. Moving forward, we should continue this practice to avoid a huge project backlog.
