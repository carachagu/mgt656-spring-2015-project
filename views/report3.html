Sprint 3 served as a wrap-up of the project for the Carachugu Ninjas. As the team completed and passed all 41 mocha tests during sprint 2, we decided to focus our effort on the ‘curveballs’ and fine-tuning the design of our website. Among the ‘curveballs,’ the team had a difficult time getting the experiments to work. We did server-side implementation and wrote a code within our controllers to randomly show either variation 1 or variation 2 of the experiments. This was working perfectly since we saw the "RSVP" in the event links alternately be shown in a random fashion. The donate link also alternately showed the words "DONATE" and "SUPPORT". However, the experiments in Google Analytics were not getting the experiment data. We tried so many things and also got stuck along the way. This led to us being delayed all throughout the sprint as can be seen in our burndown chart. Moreover, we thought we will finish this curveball sprint in 6 days but we ended up needing 8 days to complete. We then explored Google Analytics and broke down the results by 1) breakdown per referrer; 2) a general donate statistic w/o experiments; 3) if putting RSVP or donate makes people donate more.

Retrospective
  • Start doing: making the scrum master a full-time role
    Currently, the team relies on one group member for technical support. While the team member is an outstanding asset to the team, she inevitably takes a heavier workload. Moving forward, we should set aside the position of a scrum master to avoid overburdening the technical person.

  • Stop doing: clearing all the user stories in the initial stage
    The team finished all the user stories and passed all the mocha tests at the end of sprint 2. While this allows sufficient time for the team to deal with the curveballs, we can perhaps go with a more distributed workload across time.

  • Continue doing: update each other on unexpected delays via email
    The team just evolved in a way that team members are very good with communicating each other about unexpected delays via emails. This way, the team can adjust and re-estimate work progress before the sprint meeting and take corresponding actions. Moving forward, we should continue this practice to avoid a huge project backlog.
